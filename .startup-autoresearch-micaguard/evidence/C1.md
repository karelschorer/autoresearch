# C1: Technology can deliver
**Score: 7/10** (updated Cycle 9, DOWN from 8 after stress test)

## Evidence

### What works well for LLMs in regulatory compliance
- MiCA regulation text is well-structured, finite, and machine-readable (iXBRL format)
- Regulatory change classification (what changed, which CASP type is affected) is a well-suited LLM task
- Document summarization and impact assessment are core LLM strengths
- Harvey, Sedric, and other legal AI tools demonstrate the pattern works in production

### Stress test: accuracy and hallucination risks

**1. Legal AI error rates are high even for specialized tools**
- Stanford research: **17% error rate for Lexis+ AI**, **34% for Westlaw AI** — these are purpose-built legal tools from established vendors
- 700+ court cases worldwide involve AI hallucinations
- General-purpose models perform far worse on legal/regulatory interpretation
- As accuracy improves, hallucinations become harder to detect (auto-pilot risk)

**2. Regulatory interpretation requires judgment, not just retrieval**
- MiCA compliance is not just "find the relevant article" — it's "interpret how this standard applies to THIS firm's specific services and license"
- Jurisdictional nuances matter: NCA expectations differ across NL, DE, FR, IE
- Conflation risk: LLMs may mix MiCA requirements with similar but different regulations (PSD2, AMLD)
- EU AI Act classifies AI in legal/financial services as HIGH-RISK — requires conformity assessment, human oversight, audit trail

**3. Trust gap for compliance tooling**
- Compliance officers at regulated firms are risk-averse by definition
- A tool that generates incorrect regulatory interpretation could lead to license revocation
- Human-in-the-loop verification is NOT optional — it's a regulatory requirement (EU AI Act)
- Liability: who is responsible if MiCAGuard's AI misclassifies a regulatory change?

### Architecture that mitigates risk
- **Deterministic layer:** Exact matching of ESMA standard numbers, article references, and deadline dates — no AI hallucination possible
- **Classification layer:** LLM classifies which CASP types and services are affected — can be verified against structured ESMA metadata
- **Impact assessment layer:** LLM generates draft impact analysis — MUST be reviewed by human expert before delivery
- **Hybrid approach:** Same as ClaimPilot learned — deterministic matching + semantic analysis + human review

### Why dropped to 7
The technology CAN deliver, but the accuracy bar in financial regulation is extremely high and current legal AI tools have documented 17-34% error rates. EU AI Act makes human oversight mandatory. MiCAGuard cannot be "AI delivers verdict" — it must be "AI drafts analysis, human expert validates." This is still valuable (80% time savings) but limits the scalability and pure-SaaS economics. Not a 6 because the regulatory change tracking use case (what changed, who's affected, what's the deadline) is more structured and less judgment-heavy than full legal interpretation.
