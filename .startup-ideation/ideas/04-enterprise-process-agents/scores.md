# FlowAgent — Scores (Cycle 0 Baseline)

**Total: 62/100**

## A1: Problem is real — 8/10
Enterprise back-office process automation is a massive pain. Airlines alone spend billions on operations. RPA failure rates are documented (30-50% of RPA projects fail to deliver expected ROI per Forrester). The gap between documented SOPs and actual execution is real. The problem is clearly significant and well-documented.

## A2: Problem is growing — 7/10
AI agent capabilities are improving rapidly, making more complex processes automatable. However, enterprises are cautious adopters. The growth is driven by technology capability improvement, not market demand growth. Moderate trajectory.

## B1: Alternatives inadequate — 6/10
RPA (UiPath, Automation Anywhere) is a EUR 10B+ market. These vendors are aggressively adding AI capabilities (UiPath Autopilot, AA Generative AI). Microsoft Copilot is targeting the same use cases. Competing against well-funded incumbents who are adding AI to existing platforms. The "agentic" angle is novel but being adopted by every major automation vendor.

## B2: Gap exists — 6/10
The gap between "AI can read SOPs" and "AI can reliably execute multi-step processes across legacy systems" is real. No one has fully cracked this yet. But it's unclear whether a startup can solve what UiPath with EUR 1B+ R&D budget hasn't — the problem may be genuinely hard, not just underserved.

## C1: Technology can deliver — 5/10
AI agents executing multi-step enterprise processes reliably is at the frontier of current capability. Error rates, hallucination, and edge case handling in production enterprise environments are unsolved problems. Karel can build a PoC but production reliability at enterprise scale is a major question mark. This is technically the hardest idea on the list.

## C2: Wedge works — 7/10
Airline IROPS automation is a specific, high-value wedge. Disruption costs are quantifiable (EUR 50-200M/year per airline). The wedge is narrow enough to be buildable but valuable enough to justify enterprise pricing.

## D1: Market reachable — 4/10
European airlines with 50+ aircraft: maybe 30-40 carriers. Very small TAM for the beachhead. Enterprise sales cycles are 6-18 months. Karel's AF-KLM connection is one warm intro, not a channel. Expanding beyond airlines requires rebuilding domain expertise for each vertical.

## D2: Unit economics — 7/10
At EUR 20-50K/month per process, the value is clear if it works: a single avoided IROPS incident can save EUR 100K+. But the implementation cost (EUR 100-200K) and long sales cycle mean slow time-to-revenue. High ACV but slow velocity.

## E1: Team advantage — 6/10
Karel knows AF-KLM processes, which is a genuine (narrow) domain advantage. But airline operations is a specialized industry with entrenched vendors and long procurement cycles. Jeroen's fintech background doesn't help here. One warm intro to AF-KLM is valuable but exhaustible. Moderate fit.

## E2: Timing catalyst — 6/10
AI agent capabilities improving is a general trend, not a specific catalyst. No regulatory deadline, no market event creating urgency. Airlines are always interested in IROPS cost reduction but there's no "buy now" trigger. Weak timing.
