# CrewAI Ops — Scores (Cycle 0 Baseline)

**Total: 57/100**

## A1: Problem is real — 9/10
Airlines lose EUR 50-200M/year to IROPS. Crew duty-time violations cause cancellations. The pain is massive, well-documented, and quantifiable. Every airline COO lists operational resilience as a top priority. This is one of the most clearly documented problems on the list.

## A2: Problem is growing — 6/10
Air traffic is growing (post-COVID recovery), increasing disruption frequency. Climate change increases weather-related disruptions. But the problem has existed for decades — airlines have always had IROPS. Growth is incremental, not step-function.

## B1: Alternatives inadequate — 5/10
Jeppesen (Boeing), AIMS, IBS Software, and Sabre all offer crew management and operations solutions. These are legacy systems but they work and are deeply integrated. Airlines are investing heavily in upgrading these systems. Newer players like Assaia (airport ops AI) and Cirium (flight data) are entering adjacent spaces. The market has entrenched incumbents with deep airline relationships.

## B2: Gap exists — 6/10
Real-time AI-powered disruption management is an emerging capability that legacy tools don't do well. The gap is real but closing — Jeppesen is adding AI features, and airlines are building in-house AI teams. The window for a startup to capture this gap is narrowing.

## C1: Technology can deliver — 5/10
Crew optimization is a complex combinatorial problem with hard regulatory constraints (EASA flight time limitations). LLMs are not great at optimization — this requires OR/constraint solving + ML prediction. Karel would need to combine multiple technical approaches. Integration with airline systems (Amadeus, Altea, GDS) is deeply complex. Technically the hardest build on the list.

## C2: Wedge works — 6/10
Crew duty-time violation prevention is a specific wedge but requires deep integration with airline scheduling systems from day one. Not a lightweight entry point — airlines won't trust crew scheduling to an untested tool. The wedge requires more integration depth than typical SaaS.

## D1: Market reachable — 3/10
European airlines with 50+ aircraft: ~30-40 carriers. Tiny TAM. Enterprise sales cycles of 12-18 months. Single warm intro to AF-KLM. Aviation procurement is relationship-driven with long vendor qualification processes. Very hard to reach at startup pace.

## D2: Unit economics — 7/10
At EUR 20-50K/month, the ROI is clear if the product delivers (IROPS savings of EUR 50-200M/year). But implementation cost (EUR 100-200K) and 12-18 month sales cycle mean 2+ years to meaningful revenue. Slow velocity despite high ACV.

## E1: Team advantage — 5/10
Karel was a Product Owner at AF-KLM, not an operations specialist. Knowing enterprise processes is different from deep crew scheduling/IROPS domain expertise. Jeroen's fintech background is irrelevant here. One AF-KLM connection is a starting point, not a competitive advantage. The advantage is thin.

## E2: Timing catalyst — 4/10
No specific catalyst. Airlines always want better operations. AI capability is improving (general trend). No regulatory deadline, no market event. The opportunity has existed for years and will exist for years. Weak timing.
